Cat Feeding 101

A web-based instructional unit designed for novice cat owners with little or no pre-knowledge for cats. During this 45-minute e-course, students will learn how to select commercial cat food plus how to design homemade cat food, and get ready for feeding their first feline friend.

Design Challenges

Three Learning Barriers for Novice Cat Owners

“You are what you eat”, and this is equally true for our feline friends. Indeed, cat feeding is one of the most important expenses of feline guardianship and may also be the most important thing a novice cat owner need to learn. However, three big challenges exist as leaning barriers:

First, unlike other common domestic animals such as dogs, cats are obligate carnivores. Therefore, more special requirements and restrictions exist in cat feeding, which are easily ignored by novice cat owners.

Second, most cat feeding instructions are not novice friendly. When teaching how to choose commercial cat food, many cat feeding instruction would go through the whole ingredient list and talk about each terminology on it. These instructions are not so necessary for novice and will cause great cognitive load.

Third, most cat feeding instructions provide scattered knowledge components without a systematic knowledge structure, which is hard for knowledge integration and construction. Most cat feeding websites will just tell “there are 10 food cat can’t eat and you should remember them!” Novice cat owners may remember them eventually, but they will never understand why. These scattered knowledge components may cause huge blind area. For instance, novice cat owners may learn that milk is toxic from a website but still feed cat with cheese, or cake, or any other food containing lactose which is responsible for food poisoning of cats.

Cat Feeding 101 is an e-learning course designed for novice cat owners. Cat Feeding 101 will help novice cat owners to overcome their learning barriers, and will provide professional, understandable and systematic instruction to prepare them for feeding their cat friend properly.

Overview

Design Process: Backward Design

I adopted backward design model, in which I begined with a focus on the goal (learning) rather than the process (teaching). According to backward design process, I built cognitive processing model through cognitive task analysis (CTA) first, then identified learning goals based on the insights from CTA, and finally used these goals to guide assessment and instruction design.

Model Building

Model Building Process

Conducted Theoretical CTA based on literature review.

Mapped out the decision points based on Theoretical CTA result.

Conducted Empirical Prescriptive CTA with two expert cat owners (feeding cat more than 5 years).

Conducted Empirical Descriptive CTA with three novice cat owners (feeding cat less than 1 year).

Narrowed down the decision points based on Empirical CTA result.

Identified decision points and built cognitive model.

Cognitive Task Analysis

Cognitive Task Analysis (CTA) is a set of methods to specify the internal decision points when achieving a task, and help building cognitive model.

Prescriptive CTA is about expert and Descriptive CTA is about novice. Theoretical CTA provides expectations about how experts and novices are likely to engage in reasoning and learning while Empirical CTA shows how they actually do it.

Comparing results from different CTAs can reflect the difference between theory and reality, expert and novice. CTA can tell designers about what students have already known, what they need to know and what they need to learn. As a result, CTA can specify a set of decision points necessary to finish a task, which provides solid evidences for setting learning goals.

Cognitive Model

Based on the results of CTA, two cognitive models were built to show what learners need to learn to achieve the tasks.

Goals Identification

Goals Identification Process

Make a list of goals based on the decision points in the cognitive model.

Make use of ABCD model, KC, and Bloom's taxonomy to aid goal specification.

Enhance goal identification through use of a structured interview or contextual inquiry with an expert.

Assessment Design

Assessment Design Process

Come up with two sets of assessment tasks (pre-test and post-test).

Create an alignment table that indicates alignment between assessment task and learning goal.

Design feedback to correct or wrong answer.

Iterate based on data or feedback.

Pre-Post Assessment

Two sets of assesment - Pre Assessment and Post Assessment are designed for measuring the value-added in the learning progress. For better comparison, pre-test and post-test share something in common:First, pre-test and post-test are same in number (9 questions including 7 multiple choices question and 2 short answer questions). Second, the number of pre-test and post-test designed for each sub-goal are the same. Third, pre-test and post-test share similar difficulty.

Instruction Design

Instruction Design Process

Select instructional resources (e.g., slides, videos) and design activities (e.g., game, practice) in the e-learning course.

Apply e-learning principles to e-learning course design.

Implement a functioning prototype of the e-learning course.

Refine the instruction based on observation and feedback.

Multimedia Principle

Multimedia Principle indicates that students learn better from words and pictures than from words alone. Cat Feeding 101 chooses instructional materials necessarily required in learning, limits extraneous content, and combines text and relevant graphics in a proper way to support knowledge integration.

Segmenting Principle

Segmenting Principle indicates that students learn better from a multimedia lesson is presented in user-paced segments rather than as a continuous unit.

Cat feeding is a complex process including various concepts, skills and principles, which may easily cause cognitive load while learning. Cat Feeding 101 breaks down a continuous process into manageable segments. Cat Feeding 101 breaks the entire instruction into two modules, and breaks each module into smaller steps containing 2-4 knowledge components.
